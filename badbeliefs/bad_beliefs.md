# Bad Beliefs: Why They Happen to Good People
### Neil Levy | Oxford University Press, 2022

---

## Brief Overview

*Bad Beliefs* is a work of social epistemology — the philosophical study of how knowledge and belief operate within social contexts. Neil Levy, a philosopher at Macquarie University and the University of Oxford, asks a deceptively simple question: **why do intelligent, well-meaning people end up believing false, harmful, or irrational things?**

The conventional answer is that people who hold bad beliefs — conspiracy theories, pseudoscientific claims, climate change denial, vaccine misinformation — are somehow intellectually deficient: gullible, poorly educated, or cognitively biased. Levy challenges this view at its root. His central argument is that **most holders of bad beliefs are not epistemically irrational**. They are doing exactly what good epistemic agents are supposed to do — forming beliefs by deferring to apparent authorities, trusting the social signals around them, and responding to higher-order evidence about what is credible. The problem is not with the reasoner; it is with the **epistemic environment** in which they reason.

This has profound moral and political consequences. If bad beliefs are a systemic failure rather than an individual one, then blame and ridicule directed at conspiracy theorists or science deniers are not only ineffective — they are philosophically unjustified. Fixing the problem requires cleaning up the informational ecosystem, not lecturing individuals.

The book moves through several interlocking arguments: the social nature of belief, the mechanics of epistemic deference, the concept of higher-order evidence, the specific dynamics of conspiracy theories, and the ways political identity distorts epistemic practice. It concludes with a diagnosis of what has gone wrong with contemporary information environments and a call for systemic rather than individual remedies.

## Part I: The Social Architecture of Belief

### 1. We Are Not Lone Reasoners

Levy's first major move is to dismantle the image of the individual as a self-sufficient epistemic agent who gathers evidence independently and weighs it rationally. This model — call it the "Cartesian" picture — is empirically false and philosophically naive.

In reality, virtually everything we believe about science, history, medicine, law, economics, and public affairs, we believe **on the testimony of others**. We have not personally verified that the Earth is 4.5 billion years old, that smoking causes cancer, that vaccines are safe, or that the Holocaust occurred. We believe these things because we trust the people and institutions that report them. This is not laziness or credulity — it is the only viable strategy in a world of overwhelming informational complexity.

**The case of scientific knowledge:** A layperson who "believes in" climate change is not, in most cases, someone who has read and evaluated the primary literature in atmospheric physics. They believe it because they have received credible signals from multiple overlapping sources — scientists, journalists, governments, textbooks — that constitute a consensus. The belief is not irrational; it is an appropriate response to those signals.

This means that **the quality of our beliefs is heavily dependent on the quality of the social epistemic infrastructure** we are embedded in. When that infrastructure works well — when experts are trustworthy, institutions are credible, and information flows are relatively clean — deference produces mostly true beliefs. When it breaks down, deference produces false ones.

### 2. Higher-Order Evidence

One of the book's key technical concepts is **higher-order evidence**. First-order evidence is direct evidence about whether a proposition is true (e.g., a lab result, an eyewitness account). Higher-order evidence is evidence about the reliability of one's epistemic faculties or processes — evidence not about the world directly but about how well one is tracking the world.

Levy argues that higher-order evidence plays an enormous role in belief formation, often overriding or reshaping how we interpret first-order evidence. If you learn that a study you found compelling was conducted by a fraudster, you revise your credence not because the underlying data changed but because you've received evidence about the reliability of your source. If you discover that an expert you trust has been systematically deceived, that higher-order evidence appropriately shifts your confidence.

**The epistemically significant point:** When people are embedded in an information environment flooded with misleading higher-order evidence — fake experts, manufactured controversies, false claims of consensus, apparent institutional corruption — they respond rationally to that evidence by discounting legitimate authorities and elevating illegitimate ones. The misinformation doesn't just deceive at the level of facts; it corrupts the machinery people use to evaluate facts.

This is why simply "presenting the evidence" rarely works against entrenched false beliefs. The holders of those beliefs often have higher-order reasons (however spuriously obtained) to distrust the source presenting that evidence.

## Part II: Why Deference Goes Wrong

### 3. The Problem of Epistemic Trust

We cannot verify most of what we believe, so we must decide **whom to trust**. Levy examines the cues people use to identify credible sources:

- **Credentials and institutional affiliation** — degrees, peer-reviewed publications, university positions
- **Apparent consensus** — do many independent sources agree?
- **Track record** — has this source been reliable before?
- **Social signals** — do people I respect or identify with trust this source?

These are generally sensible heuristics. The trouble is that each can be gamed or corrupted:

- Credentials can be fabricated or irrelevant (a doctor speaking outside their specialty)
- Manufactured controversy can simulate dissent where consensus actually exists (the tobacco industry's deliberate strategy)
- Track records can be selectively presented
- Social signals can be manipulated through bots, astroturfing, and algorithmic amplification

**The tobacco playbook:** Levy draws on the well-documented history of the tobacco industry's disinformation campaign — studied at length by Naomi Oreskes and Erik Conway in *Merchants of Doubt* — as an archetypal case. Tobacco companies knew their product was lethal from the 1950s onward. Rather than suppressing the evidence directly, they funded a systematic campaign to manufacture the appearance of scientific controversy. They did not need to fool scientists; they needed only to give ordinary people sufficient higher-order reasons to think the science was unsettled. This was epistemically devastating and morally catastrophic, and it established the template replicated later by the fossil fuel industry on climate change, and by various actors on vaccine safety.

### 4. Signaling and Identity-Protective Cognition

Levy engages with a body of empirical work — much of it from Dan Kahan's "cultural cognition" research — showing that on politically contested topics, people tend to form beliefs that align with their group identity rather than with evidence. Better-educated, more analytically sophisticated individuals do not converge on correct beliefs; if anything, they are *better* at marshaling evidence selectively in defense of their group's position.

This might seem to undermine Levy's case that bad-belief-holders are rational. But he offers a subtler reading: in many social contexts, **what a belief signals matters more than whether it is accurate**. Beliefs are not merely representations of the world; they are social acts that express allegiance, value, and identity. Holding the "wrong" belief about climate change or vaccines within a tight-knit community carries real social costs — ostracism, loss of status, damaged relationships — that can rationally outweigh the abstract epistemic benefits of being correct.

**Case: Climate change denial in fossil-fuel-dependent communities.** Research consistently finds that climate skepticism is higher in communities economically dependent on fossil fuels. This is not simply ignorance — many of these communities include technically literate people. Rather, accepting the reality of climate change would be socially and economically costly. The denial is at least partly identity-protective, a way of preserving coherence with one's community and livelihood. Levy does not excuse the belief as *true*, but he argues it is not a simple failure of rationality either; it is a response to genuine social pressures.

### 5. Conspiracy Theories: A Special Case

Levy devotes careful attention to conspiracy theories because they are especially resistant to standard debunking and because they involve a distinctive epistemic structure.

**What makes conspiracy theories distinctive:** Unlike ordinary false beliefs (e.g., a factual error), conspiracy theories typically incorporate a theory about *why the truth is being suppressed*. This makes them self-sealing: any evidence against the conspiracy can be reinterpreted as evidence of the conspiracy's reach. The claim that vaccines cause autism is not just a false claim about autism; it is paired with a narrative that pharmaceutical companies, regulatory agencies, and mainstream medicine are all complicit in hiding the truth.

This self-sealing structure means that conspiracy theories are extremely robust against disconfirming evidence, because disconfirmation is already theorized into the conspiracy itself. Showing someone more evidence does not help; it may, by further engaging their attention, entrench the belief more deeply (this is sometimes called the "backfire effect," though the empirical status of that effect is disputed).

**Why do people adopt conspiracy theories?** Levy's account is multi-causal:

- *Mistrust of institutions:* When people have genuine grounds for mistrusting official sources — and historically marginalized communities often do, for documented reasons — they become more receptive to conspiratorial alternatives.
- *Pattern-seeking and agency detection:* Humans are naturally inclined to see intentional patterns, especially in significant or harmful events. This is a cognitive feature, not a bug — it generally serves us well — but it misfires in complex, impersonal systems.
- *Social embedding:* Conspiracy theories circulate through networks where believing them confers social rewards and status. People who share conspiracy content online often do so for social reasons as much as epistemic ones.
- *Epistemic vulnerability:* People who feel epistemically powerless — who feel they lack access to real information about how the world works — are drawn to conspiracy narratives that promise special, "hidden" knowledge.

**Case: The anti-vaccine movement.** Levy examines the vaccine-hesitancy movement as a paradigm case of how these factors interact. The foundational piece of misinformation — Andrew Wakefield's fraudulent 1998 Lancet paper linking the MMR vaccine to autism — was retracted, and Wakefield was stripped of his medical license. Yet the belief persists robustly. Why?

Because by the time of the retraction, a self-sustaining social community had formed around the belief. For parents — especially mothers — of autistic children, the anti-vaccine movement offered community, meaning, apparent explanation, and a sense of agency (advocating for their children). The retraction was absorbed into the conspiratorial structure: Wakefield was a hero persecuted by pharmaceutical interests. The social and psychological functions of the belief made it deeply resistant to purely evidentiary correction.

Moreover, the anti-vaccine community rapidly became embedded in the broader ecosystem of alternative health, distrust of "mainstream medicine," and suspicion of regulatory capture — an ecosystem that was itself the product of genuine historical grievances (overprescription of opioids, conflicts of interest in drug trials) now generalized far beyond appropriate application.

## Part III: Political Polarization and Partisan Epistemology

### 6. When Politics Corrupts Epistemic Practice

One of the book's most politically charged sections examines how partisan identity systematically distorts epistemic practice. Levy is careful not to make a symmetric "both sides" argument — he acknowledges that in the contemporary United States, science denial is heavily concentrated on one side of the political spectrum — but his analytical framework is, in principle, applicable across the political landscape.

The key mechanism is what Levy calls **partisan epistemic tribalism**: the tendency to accept or reject claims based primarily on their source and their alignment with group identity, rather than on evidence. This is not simple irrationality; given that we must use social cues to navigate information, and given that political identity is a salient social cue, using political affiliation as an epistemic filter has a certain rationality to it. The problem is that it becomes systematically misleading in a polarized environment.

**Case: COVID-19 and political belief formation.** Levy discusses the COVID-19 pandemic as an acute case study in how political identity shaped epistemic responses to a shared empirical situation. Early in the pandemic, beliefs about the severity of COVID, the efficacy of masks, and the legitimacy of lockdowns tracked political identity with remarkable precision. Survey data consistently showed that party affiliation was a stronger predictor of COVID-related beliefs than geographic proximity to outbreaks or personal contact with infected individuals.

Levy's explanation is not that Republicans were uniquely irrational. Rather, they were responding to a different information environment — one in which the dominant sources they trusted (right-wing media, Donald Trump, skeptical commentators) consistently downplayed the virus's severity, while the sources they distrusted (mainstream media, Democratic politicians, "liberal" scientists) emphasized it. Within their epistemic community, accepting the dominant liberal framing of COVID would have been a costly signal of defection. Discounting it was, by the logic of their information environment, the epistemically appropriate response.

This does not excuse COVID misinformation or the genuine harm it caused. Levy's point is that the solution cannot be to individually convince each skeptic through evidence, because the problem is structural. The credibility of legitimate scientific communication had been systematically eroded through years of partisan epistemology.

### 7. Fake News and Information Pollution

Levy examines the phenomenon of "fake news" — deliberately fabricated or misleading news stories circulated through social media — as a deliberate strategy of **epistemic pollution** rather than mere misinformation.

The goal of much fake news is not to convince people of specific false propositions. It is to generate a pervasive atmosphere of uncertainty and distrust — to convince people that *nothing can be known* with confidence, that all sources have agendas, that truth is inaccessible. When this succeeds, it does not create believers in alternative narratives so much as it creates cynics who have withdrawn from the epistemic commons. A population of cynics — unable to distinguish reliable from unreliable information, inclined to believe all claims are equally suspect — is just as susceptible to manipulation as a population of true believers, and in some ways more so.

**Case: Russian information operations.** Levy refers to well-documented Russian disinformation campaigns — in the 2016 U.S. election and across European democracies — as examples of sophisticated epistemic pollution. The campaigns did not uniformly promote any single narrative; they promoted contradictory narratives simultaneously, amplified existing social divisions, and spread content designed to generate outrage and distrust. The goal was not to make Americans believe any particular thing, but to make them trust institutions less, trust each other less, and lose confidence in their ability to collectively determine political truth.

## Part IV: Moral Responsibility and the Ethics of Belief

### 8. Are Believers Blameworthy?

A central ethical question in the book is whether holders of bad beliefs are morally responsible for them. Levy's answer is nuanced: **moral blame is usually inappropriate, even when the beliefs are harmful and even when the believer is wrong**.

The standard assumption is that adults are responsible for their beliefs — that if you believe something false, especially if it has harmful consequences, you are culpable for having failed to reason carefully enough. Levy challenges this by applying standard conditions for moral responsibility (control, alternative possibilities, reasons-responsiveness) to the case of belief.

For blame to be appropriate, a person must have had a reasonable alternative available, and must have had the epistemic resources to recognize that alternative. For someone who was raised in a community where a false belief is universal, whose trusted sources all confirm it, and who has no access to credible counter-information, the conditions for blame are typically not met. They believe what their environment has made it rational to believe.

**The case of young-Earth creationists:** Levy uses creationism as a recurring example. Approximately 40% of Americans believe that God created humans in their present form within the past 10,000 years, contrary to the overwhelming consensus of geology, biology, genetics, and paleontology. Are these people irrational? Many are not; they are responding to a coherent religious community in which this belief is universal, in which apparent authorities confirm it, and in which accepting evolution is framed as an act of apostasy and moral betrayal. The higher-order evidence available to them within their community supports the belief. Blaming them individually is both unfair and counterproductive.

This does not mean all belief is blameless. Levy identifies cases where blame is warranted:

- **When someone has clear access to countervailing evidence but deliberately avoids it** (willful ignorance)
- **When a person's epistemic position confers special responsibilities** (political leaders, medical professionals, scientists who deny their own field's consensus)
- **When the harmful consequences of the belief are easily foreseeable and serious**, and the person has the resources to investigate

Even in these cases, Levy suggests, blame should be calibrated to actual epistemic opportunity, not simply to outcome.

### 9. Epistemic Injustice and Structural Power

Levy engages with Miranda Fricker's concept of **epistemic injustice** — the wrong done to someone specifically in their capacity as a knower. Testimonial injustice occurs when someone's credibility is unfairly deflated because of prejudice (race, gender, class). Hermeneutical injustice occurs when someone lacks the conceptual resources to understand or articulate their own experience.

These concepts are relevant because they explain part of why some communities are more epistemically vulnerable than others. Communities that have historically been lied to by authorities — government experiments on Black Americans, medical paternalism toward women, colonial misrepresentation of indigenous populations — have rational grounds for heightened institutional distrust. When their distrust becomes generalized beyond its appropriate scope, this is an effect of prior epistemic injustice, not a free-floating failure of rationality.

**Case: Vaccine hesitancy in Black American communities.** Vaccine hesitancy is significantly elevated in African American communities in the United States. This is routinely attributed to "misinformation," but Levy's framework demands a deeper analysis. The hesitancy has historically traceable roots: the Tuskegee syphilis experiment (1932–1972), in which the U.S. Public Health Service deliberately withheld treatment from Black men with syphilis while telling them they were being treated; ongoing documented racial disparities in medical care; and a general pattern of institutional betrayal. Distrust of government health agencies in this community is not irrational — it is a reasonable response to documented history. The challenge is that this legitimate and historically grounded distrust has been exploited and amplified by anti-vaccine disinformation specifically targeting Black communities. The solution must include genuine institutional accountability, not just better messaging.

## Part V: What Is to Be Done

### 10. Individual Remedies and Their Limits

Much of the conventional response to misinformation focuses on individual-level interventions: education in critical thinking, "prebunking" or "inoculation" techniques, media literacy curricula, fact-checking services. Levy reviews this literature and acknowledges that some individual interventions have demonstrated effects in controlled studies.

However, he is skeptical that individual-level interventions can address the scale of the problem for several reasons:

- **Effects are often small, short-lived, and difficult to scale.** Telling someone that a claim is false may reduce their credence in that claim in the moment, but this effect decays quickly and does not transfer to related claims.
- **"Inoculation" requires advance notice.** Prebunking (exposing people to weakened versions of misinformation before they encounter the real thing) is effective in experiments, but presupposes that we know in advance what misinformation people will encounter.
- **Individual interventions don't address the structural causes.** Even excellent critical-thinking skills provide limited protection if the information environment is saturated with sophisticated, high-volume disinformation.
- **Motivation and identity:** People who are motivated to maintain a belief for identity-protective reasons will use their critical thinking skills in the service of that motivation — as tools for rationalization rather than honest inquiry.

### 11. Structural and Systemic Remedies

Levy's preferred remedies are structural. If bad beliefs arise from a corrupted epistemic environment, fixing the environment is the right target.

**Platform regulation and algorithmic reform:** Social media platforms, through engagement-maximizing algorithms, systematically amplify outrage, polarization, and sensationalism — not because they intend to spread misinformation, but because false, emotionally provocative content generates more engagement than accurate, measured content. This creates a structural bias toward misinformation at the infrastructure level. Levy supports regulatory interventions that require platforms to redesign their recommendation systems with epistemic quality, not just engagement, as a design criterion.

**Strengthening epistemic institutions:** Universities, scientific bodies, public health agencies, and quality journalism are the load-bearing institutions of the epistemic commons. Years of defunding, politicization, and delegitimization have weakened them. Restoring their credibility requires both institutional reform (addressing genuine conflicts of interest, improving transparency) and cultural and political protection from bad-faith attacks.

**Epistemic diversity with structure:** Levy is nuanced about the value of epistemic diversity. Diversity of perspectives can be epistemically valuable when it generates genuine disagreement among people reasoning carefully. But not all diversity is created equal — manufactured controversy (fake diversity designed to simulate disagreement where none exists among experts) is epistemically destructive. The goal is to foster genuine deliberation while identifying and countering the simulation of deliberation.

**Addressing underlying social conditions:** Because bad beliefs often arise from genuine grievances — economic insecurity, political powerlessness, institutional betrayal — purely epistemic interventions are insufficient. Addressing the social conditions that make people epistemically vulnerable is an essential part of the solution. This means reducing inequality, restoring institutional accountability, and rebuilding the social trust that makes epistemic cooperation possible.

---

## Conclusion: The Epistemic Commons

Levy closes with the observation that healthy collective belief formation is a **public good** — one that, like other public goods, is susceptible to both market failure and deliberate sabotage, and that requires collective institutions to maintain. The individualist model of epistemic responsibility — each person is responsible for their own beliefs, and bad beliefs reflect individual failures — is not just inaccurate; it actively impedes the systemic responses that the problem requires.

The book is ultimately a call for a more structurally sophisticated approach to epistemology and to politics. Understanding why bad beliefs happen to good people — not because those people are stupid or weak, but because they are embedded in systems that systematically mislead them — is the necessary first step toward doing something about it.

## Key Concepts Summary

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Higher-order evidence** | Evidence about the reliability of one's own reasoning or sources, rather than about the world directly |
| **Epistemic deference** | Forming beliefs by accepting the testimony of apparent authorities rather than evaluating evidence directly |
| **Epistemic pollution** | Deliberate corruption of the information environment to generate confusion and distrust rather than specific false beliefs |
| **Identity-protective cognition** | Using cognitive resources to defend beliefs that align with one's social identity rather than to track truth |
| **Testimonial injustice** | Unfairly deflating someone's credibility as a knower due to prejudice |
| **Epistemic commons** | The shared social infrastructure of knowledge production and transmission |
| **Manufactured controversy** | Deliberate simulation of expert disagreement where genuine consensus exists |
| **Self-sealing belief** | A belief structured so that apparent disconfirmation is reinterpreted as further evidence for it |
